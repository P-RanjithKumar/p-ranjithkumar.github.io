The farewell wasn't cinematic ‚Äî it was messy, loud, and heartbreakingly human.

At Chennai airport, I tried to be brave. But when the flight got delayed (of course it did), anxiety staged its own drama. Still, when I finally hugged my friends one last time, I realized ‚Äî this chaos is love.

Coimbatore ‚Üí Mumbai (IndiGo) ‚Üí Istanbul ‚Üí Dublin (Turkish Airlines).

"Yes Amma, I'm alive. No, I didn't lose my passport. Yet."

Hazelwood Student Village was my new "home." It was clean, cozy, and painfully expensive. The first grocery trip nearly gave me a heart attack ‚Äî rice cost more than chai!

üåßÔ∏è Arrival: Ireland Hits Differently

Ireland doesn't welcome you gently ‚Äî it slaps you awake with wind and sideways rain.

Dublin city center buzzed with trams, polite strangers, and bookstores that begged for rainy-day reading. Temple Bar, St. Stephen's Green, Grafton Street ‚Äî all postcard-perfect.

But nothing prepared me for the math of grocery shopping. Bananas shouldn't cost that much.

First Impressions: Culture Shock in Real Time

  • The Weather: Rain isn't an event here; it's a lifestyle. I learned to carry an umbrella everywhere, even on sunny days.
  • The Accents: Irish English is beautiful but bewildering. "Grand" means everything from "good" to "okay" to "I'm not sure either."
  • The People: Surprisingly warm for such cold weather. Random strangers helped me find my way more times than Google Maps did.
  • The Food: Let's not talk about what I paid for vegetables. But discovering Irish breakfast (full fry-up) made up for some of the pain.

üéì University Life: Panic, People, and Little Wins

DCU doesn't play around. Miss one week, and you're already behind a mountain of code. Between assignments, plagiarism warnings, and group projects with five accents at once, I sometimes wondered if I accidentally enrolled in a global experiment.

Still ‚Äî there were bright spots:

  • Cricket club on rainy weekends ‚Äî finding home in the least expected places
  • Engineering club coffee breaks that felt like therapy sessions
  • Job rejections that stung, but toughened me up
  • Late-night coding sessions where broken code somehow became breakthroughs
  • International potlucks where I finally got to show off my (admittedly limited) cooking skills

The Howth Cliffs Moment

And then ‚Äî Howth Cliffs. Standing there with friends, hair flying in the Atlantic wind, laughing for no reason ‚Äî I realized Dublin was quietly becoming home.

Sometimes the best moments aren't planned. They're just Tuesday afternoons when you decide to take a DART to the edge of the sea and remember why you took this leap.

üå± Lessons from the Journey

Six months in, here's what I've learned:

  • Home is portable: It's in WhatsApp calls at 2 AM, in a friend's laugh, in the smell of chai you finally learned to make right.
  • Discomfort breeds growth: Every awkward moment, every cultural confusion, every "I have no idea what I'm doing" ‚Äî they're all building blocks.
  • Community matters: Finding your tribe in a foreign land isn't just nice, it's necessary.
  • Failure is data: Every rejected job application, every failed assignment draft ‚Äî they're teaching me resilience I never knew I needed.
  • The little things matter: A sunny day in Dublin. A perfectly cooked meal. A message from home. These aren't small joys; they're lifelines.

üå¶Ô∏è Looking Back: The Real Growth Story

Homesickness still visits sometimes, usually at midnight with a cup of overbrewed tea. But so does joy ‚Äî in little victories, in shared laughter, in seeing how far I've come.

The Ranjith who left Coimbatore and the one writing this are different people. One was nervous, uncertain, clutching a boarding pass like a lifeline. The other is still nervous, still uncertain, but now knows that's okay.

"Growth doesn't announce itself with fireworks. It whispers in the quiet moments when you realize you've stopped Googling 'How to adult in a foreign country' because you're just... doing it."

üí≠ Would I Do It Again?

If you ask me now, "Would you do it all again?"

Because somewhere between the rain, the ramen dinners, the random friendships, and the relentless assignments ‚Äî I found myself growing, laughing, and learning that sometimes, you have to let life blow you off your feet...

...and enjoy the flight.

üìù To Future International Students

If you're reading this from your hometown, passport application in hand, wondering if you should take the leap ‚Äî here's my advice:

  • Do it. The paperwork will stress you out. The goodbyes will break your heart. The first month will feel impossible.
  • But do it anyway. Because on the other side of that anxiety is a version of you that's braver, wiser, and infinitely more interesting.
  • Pack light, but bring patience. You'll need more of the latter than the former.
  • Budget ruthlessly. Every euro counts. Every. Single. One.
  • Say yes to adventures. Even when you're tired, broke, or both. Some of the best memories come from the plans you almost canceled.
  • Call home often. They miss you more than they say.
  • Be kind to yourself. You're doing something incredibly hard. Give yourself credit for showing up every day.

This journey from Coimbatore to Dublin isn't just about geography. It's about transformation ‚Äî messy, chaotic, beautiful transformation.

And I'm here for every rain-soaked, euro-counting, lesson-learning moment of it.
